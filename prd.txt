PART ONE: PRODUCT REQUIREMENTS DOCUMENT (PRD)
Section 1: Product Foundation
1.1 Product Vision
VectorShift is a global climate-health intelligence platform that visualizes how rising temperatures and shifting precipitation patterns are expanding the geographic range of disease-carrying vectors — mosquitoes, ticks, and sandflies — into regions where they have never existed before.

The platform transforms raw NASA climate data, WHO disease surveillance records, and published vector biology research into an interactive, visual, decision-support tool that health officials, researchers, journalists, and policymakers can use without any technical background.

Vision Statement:

VectorShift makes the invisible visible — showing governments, researchers, and citizens where the next epidemic risk is quietly building, before the first person gets sick.

Mission Statement:

To democratize access to climate-health intelligence by combining freely available scientific data into a single, beautiful, actionable platform that helps the world prepare for tomorrow's disease threats today.

1.2 Product Goals
Goal	Description	Success Metric
Awareness	Make climate-driven disease risk understandable to non-scientists	Time on site > 4 minutes average
Utility	Provide genuine research value to public health professionals	Downloads of data/reports per month
Credibility	Establish VectorShift as a trusted, science-backed resource	Citation in academic or news contexts
Reach	Serve users across multiple countries and disciplines	Users from 30+ countries within 6 months
Impact	Influence preparedness decisions at organizational level	Documented use by NGO or government body
1.3 Product Principles
These principles guide every design and product decision:

Principle 1 — Clarity over complexity
Every feature must be understandable to a health official who is not a data scientist. If something requires a manual to understand, it is too complex.

Principle 2 — Data honesty
Always communicate what the data can and cannot tell you. Show confidence intervals. Label projections as projections. Never overstate certainty.

Principle 3 — Speed of insight
A user should be able to find a meaningful insight within 60 seconds of arriving on the platform. No onboarding required for core features.

Principle 4 — Visual storytelling
The platform should feel like an editorial data experience, not a government database. Every chart, map, and number should tell part of a story.

Principle 5 — Earned trust
Every data point links to its source. Every methodology is explained. Users can verify everything.

VectorShift serves five distinct user groups with different needs, different technical backgrounds, and different reasons to visit.

Section 2: User Personas
Persona 1 — The Public Health Official
Name: Dr. Amira Hassan
Location: Cairo, Egypt
Role: Epidemiologist at the Egyptian Ministry of Health
Age: 38
Technical Level: Moderate — comfortable with Excel, basic GIS, reads WHO reports

Context:
Amira tracks disease trends in Egypt and neighboring countries. She has seen dengue cases emerging in coastal regions and needs to make a case to her ministry for increased vector control funding before a major outbreak occurs.

Goals:

Understand whether Egypt's climate is becoming more suitable for dengue mosquitoes
Compare Egypt's risk trajectory with neighboring countries
Generate a report or evidence package she can present to decision-makers
Understand which regions within her country are most at risk
Frustrations:

WHO data is in PDFs and spreadsheets that are hard to compare
No single tool shows climate data alongside disease data
Existing tools require GIS expertise she doesn't have
Reports take weeks to compile manually
How VectorShift Helps:
She opens the platform, selects dengue, selects Egypt, and sees a clear risk timeline from 1990 to 2050. She sees the coastal governorates highlighted as highest risk. She exports a one-page summary. She presents it the next day.

Quoted Need:

"I need to show my minister a clear picture of the risk before it becomes a crisis, not after."

Persona 2 — The Climate/Health Researcher
Name: James Okonkwo
Location: Nairobi, Kenya / London, UK (PhD student)
Role: Doctoral researcher studying climate change and infectious disease
Age: 27
Technical Level: High — uses R, Python, reads primary literature

Context:
James is writing his thesis on how climate change is affecting malaria distribution in sub-Saharan Africa. He needs data across multiple countries and decades. Currently he spends weeks collecting and cleaning data from different sources.

Goals:

Access cleaned, standardized climate and disease data for African countries
Explore correlations between temperature changes and disease incidence
Download data in usable formats for his own analysis
Find visual references for his thesis presentations
Frustrations:

Data is scattered across NASA, WHO, and country-level portals in incompatible formats
Most platforms are either too simple (no raw data) or too complex (GIS software)
Difficult to compare countries because data uses different time periods and units
How VectorShift Helps:
James uses the Data Explorer to filter malaria data across 20 African countries, downloads it as CSV, and uses it in his R analysis. He also screenshots the trend visualizations for his thesis presentation.

Quoted Need:

"I spend more time finding and cleaning data than actually doing research."

Page 8: Methodology ( /methodology )
Purpose: Establish scientific credibility. This page is what separates VectorShift from a student project and makes it feel like a real research platform.

Methodology Page Content
Section 1 — How Suitability Is Calculated

Plain language explanation:

text

HOW WE MEASURE RISK

VectorShift calculates a "habitat suitability score" for 
each disease vector in each country for each year. This 
score represents how suitable the climate conditions are 
for that vector to survive and reproduce.

A score of 1.0 means conditions are perfect for the 
vector. A score of 0.0 means conditions cannot support 
the vector at all.

The score is calculated using temperature, humidity, 
and rainfall data from NASA satellites, compared against 
published biological thresholds from peer-reviewed research.

PART THREE: DATA & CONTENT SPECIFICATION
Section 9: Data Architecture
9.1 Data Sources — Complete Specification
Source 1: NASA POWER (Primary Climate Data)
What it provides:
Monthly averages for temperature (T2M), minimum temperature (T2M_MIN), humidity (RH2M), and precipitation (PRECTOTCORR) for any location on Earth from 1981 to present.

Coverage: Global, every point on Earth

Resolution: 0.5° × 0.5° grid (approximately 55km × 55km at equator)

For MVP: We use country-level aggregated values. NASA POWER provides direct country-level aggregation via their API.

Update frequency: Monthly (new data ~2 months behind current date)

API approach for data collection:

text

Endpoint: https://power.larc.nasa.gov/api/temporal/monthly/country
Parameters:
  - parameters: T2M,T2M_MIN,RH2M,PRECTOTCORR
  - community: AG
  - country: [ISO3 code]
  - start: 1981
  - end: 2023
  - format: JSON

Rate limit: No authentication required, reasonable use
Strategy: Run collection script once per country, store results in DB.
195 countries × 1 request each = 195 requests total. One-time operation.
License: NASA Open Data — free to use, attribute NASA

Source 2: WHO Global Health Observatory (GHO)
What it provides:
Disease incidence and mortality data by country and year for dengue, malaria, leishmaniasis, and others.

Coverage: Country-level, most WHO member states

Resolution: Country-level

Update frequency: Annual

9.3 Suitability Score Calculation Algorithm
This is the core logic of VectorShift. It must be fully specified before building.

text

SUITABILITY SCORE ALGORITHM
────────────────────────────

Input:
  - country_climate: { T2M_mean, T2M_min_coldest, RH2M_mean, PREC_annual, 
                       suitable_months, altitude_median }
  - disease_parameters: (from VECTOR_PARAMETERS above)

Output:
  - suitability_score: 0.0 to 1.0

Steps:

STEP 1 — Temperature Score (weight: 0.35)
  IF T2M_mean < param.temperature.min OR T2M_mean > param.temperature.max:
    temp_score = 0
  ELIF T2M_mean >= param.temperature.optimal_min AND T2M_mean <= param.temperature.optimal_max:
    temp_score = 1.0
  ELSE:
    temp_score = linear interpolation toward 0 from optimal to min/max boundary

STEP 2 — Winter Survival Score (weight: 0.25)
  IF T2M_min_coldest < param.temperature.winter_min:
    winter_score = 0  ← Vector cannot overwinter, cannot establish
  ELIF T2M_min_coldest > param.temperature.winter_min + 5:
    winter_score = 1.0
  ELSE:
    winter_score = linear interpolation

STEP 3 — Humidity Score (weight: 0.20)
  IF RH2M_mean < param.humidity.min:
    humidity_score = 0
  ELIF RH2M_mean > param.humidity.min + 20:
    humidity_score = 1.0
  ELSE:
    humidity_score = linear interpolation

STEP 4 — Rainfall/Breeding Score (weight: 0.15)
  suitable_months = count of months where PREC >= param.precipitation.min_monthly
  IF suitable_months < param.precipitation.required_months:
    rain_score = 0
  ELSE:
    rain_score = min(1.0, suitable_months / (param.precipitation.required_months * 1.5))

STEP 5 — Altitude Score (weight: 0.05)
  IF altitude_median > param.altitude.max:
    altitude_score = 0
  ELIF altitude_median > param.altitude.max * 0.8:
    altitude_score = 0.5
  ELSE:
    altitude_score = 1.0

FINAL SCORE:
  suitability_score = (temp_score × 0.35) + 
                      (winter_score × 0.25) + 
                      (humidity_score × 0.20) + 
                      (rain_score × 0.15) + 
                      (altitude_score × 0.05)

  ← Result is 0.0 to 1.0

VULNERABILITY INDEX:
  vulnerability = (suitability_score × 0.40) +
                  ((1 - health_system_score) × 0.35) +
                  (population_exposure_score × 0.15) +
                  (trend_direction_score × 0.10)

10.3 Data Validation Rules
text

VALIDATION RULES
────────────────

countries:
  - iso2: exactly 2 uppercase letters
  - iso3: exactly 3 uppercase letters
  - slug: lowercase, hyphens only, no spaces
  - latitude: -90 to 90
  - longitude: -180 to 180

climate_data:
  - year: 1981 to current year
  - temp_mean_annual: -60 to 60 (°C)
  - humidity_mean: 0 to 100 (%)
  - precip values: 0 to 5000 (mm)
  - suitable_months_*: 0 to 12

suitability_scores:
  - all component scores: 0.000 to 1.000
  - suitability_score: 0.000 to 1.000
  - scenario: must be NULL (historical) or one of the valid SSP values
  - risk_level: must be one of defined enum values

disease_incidence:

10.4 Caching Strategy
text

CACHING LAYERS
──────────────

Layer 1: Database Query Cache (Supabase built-in)
  - Automatic for repeated identical queries
  - No configuration needed

Layer 2: Application Cache (Next.js)
  - Static generation for country profiles (revalidate: 86400 — once per day)
  - ISR (Incremental Static Regeneration) for country pages
  - Implemented with: export const revalidate = 86400

Layer 3: API Response Cache (Vercel Edge Cache)
  - Cache-Control: s-maxage=3600, stale-while-revalidate=86400
  - Map data endpoint: cache 1 hour (data changes rarely)
  - Country profile endpoint: cache 24 hours
  - Global trends: cache 24 hours
  - AI responses: NO cache (unique per query)

Layer 4: Browser Cache
  - GeoJSON country boundaries: Cache 7 days (rarely changes)
  - Static assets: Cache 1 year (content-hashed)
  - API responses: Cache 15 minutes with stale-while-revalidate

WHAT TO PRE-CALCULATE (not calculate on request):
  - All suitability scores: calculated once during ETL, stored in DB
  - Vulnerability index: calculated once during ETL, stored in DB
  - Global stats: calculated once per data update, stored in global_stats_cache
  - Risk classifications: calculated during ETL, stored as string

WHAT TO CALCULATE ON REQUEST:
  - Comparison between two countries (simple JOIN query, fast)
  - AI assistant responses (inherently dynamic)
  - Custom date range queries in data explorer
